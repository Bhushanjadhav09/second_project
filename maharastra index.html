Information Of Maharashtra

Maharashtra is one of India's largest commercial and industrial centres, which has led to its being called the gateway of India. Maharashtra is also famous for its culture and beauty.

Maharashtra is India's second most industrialised state contributing 20% of national industrial output. Almost 46% of the GSDP is contributed by industry. Maharashtra has software parks in many cities around the state, and is the second largest exporter of software with annual exports over ₹ 80,000 crores.

Traditional & Food of Maharastra

Attire. Traditional attire in Maharashtra includes the dhoti, and pheta for men, while a choli and nine-yard saree locally known as Navwri saree for women. Traditional attire is becoming rarer with trousers and shirts for males and five yard saree or salwar kameez for females as the popular replacements.

In many metropolitan areas, including Mumbai and Pune, fast food is popular. The most-popular forms are bhaji, vada pav, misalpav and pav bhaji. More-traditional dishes are sabudana khichadi, pohe, upma, sheera and panipuri. Most Marathi fast food and snacks are lacto-vegetarian.

language

Marathi language, Indo-Aryan language of western and central India. Its range extends from north of Mumbai down the western coast past Goa and eastward across the Deccan; in 1966 it became the official language of the state of Maharashtra. The standard form of speech is that of the city of Pune (Poona). Descended from the Mahārāṣṭrī Prākrit, Marathi has a significant literature. Books are printed in Devanāgarī script, which is also used for handwriting, although a closely related script, Modi, was also often used for handwriting until the mid-20th century. Eastern Hindi is the Indo-Aryan language most closely related to Marathi. Like Hindi, Marathi has lost most of its inflectional system to indicate case, using instead postpositions (like prepositions, only following the word) with an oblique “case” to serve the function originally filled by inflection.

Culture of Maharashtra?

The culture of Maharashtra is a rich blend of traditional customs, art forms, festivals, and cuisine. It is characterized by its diverse traditions, including classical music, dance forms like Lavani and Tamasha, vibrant arts like Warli painting, and significant festivals like Ganesh Chaturthi & Diwali. Maharashtrian culture is important because it reflects the state's diverse history and heritage. It offers a unique blend of traditions and modernity, showcasing India's cultural diversity. The state's festivals, arts, and cuisine contribute significantly to India's rich cultural tapestry.

Ganpati Celibration

Ganesh Chaturthi is a Hindu festival that celebrates the birth of Lord Ganesha, the god of wisdom, prosperity, and beginnings. It is a 10-day festival that takes place in August or September.

Birth of Lord Ganesha: The festival celebrates the birth of Lord Ganesha, the son of Parvati and Shiva. Worship: Devotees pray to Lord Ganesha and make offerings of flowers and food. Statues: Statues of Lord Ganesha are installed in decorated pandals. Processions: Processions of song and dance are held in the streets. Fasting: Some devotees observe a fast during the festival. When is it celebrated? Ganesh Chaturthi is celebrated on the fourth day of Bhadrapada, the sixth month of the Hindu calendar. The festival ends on Anant Chaturdashi, the 14th day of Bhadrapada. Where is it celebrated? Ganesh Chaturthi is celebrated across India, but is especially popular in Maharashtra, Telangana, and Karnataka. How is it celebrated? People bring home idols of Lord Ganesha and perform rituals. People immerse the idols in water at the end of the festival, which is called Ganesh Visarjan.

Diwali Celibration

Diwali is a festival of lights celebrated in India that marks the victory of good over evil. It usually takes place in October or November. Diwali is also known as Deepavali, which means "row of lamps" in Sanskrit. It is a time to celebrate new beginnings, love, sharing, and togetherness. Diwali is celebrated by Hindus, Sikhs, Jains, and Buddhists. Lighting diyas: People light oil lamps, or diyas, to dispel darkness. Decorating homes: People decorate their homes with colorful rangoli and lights. Exchanging gifts: People exchange gifts, sweets, and good wishes with friends and family. Praying: People pray to Goddess Lakshmi for wealth and prosperity. Setting off fireworks: People set off fireworks to celebrate the victory of light. Visiting: People visit friends and family. Feasting: People feast and share sweets. Wearing new clothes: People wear new clothes. Diwali celebrates the victory of good over evil. It also celebrates the birth of Lakshmi, the goddess of wealth. In Jainism, it commemorates the enlightenment of Mahavira. In North India, it celebrates the return of Lord Rama to Ayodhya after 14 years of exile.
